Why this site exists.

A working document for a working project — and what I figured out by stripping it down.

A site for a personal project should not be a portfolio and it should not be an audience-building funnel. It should be the one place where the rules, the rhythm, and the long arc all live, so the person it belongs to doesn't have to remember any of it twice.

I rebuilt this site three times in two days. The first version had live data fetches, content calendar widgets, per-script HTML pages, and dashboards that auto-refreshed. The second version was cleaner but still tried to be a tool. The third version — this one — gave up on being a tool and committed to being a document.

The shift mattered. A tool wants to be checked. A document wants to be returned to. They are different things.

What the site is for, in one sentence

A static reference for the working brain — voice, life rhythm, money plan, the long arc — that gets edited by hand when something material shifts and otherwise stays out of the way.

That's it. The filming materials live offline as PDFs because a 45-second script does not belong in a browser tab. The content calendar got dropped because it was a thing I'd update once and then resent every time I opened. The per-script pages got dropped because they were trying to be a CMS for an audience of one.

What stayed

Foundation, voice, hook formulas, format library, Polina arc — the brand reference that makes the work consistent. Life rhythm, meals, sleep, chores, acting practice — the personal operating manual that makes the work sustainable. Money plan, projections, quit-line math — the runway. The long arc page — the north star, for the days when the algorithm is silent.

Everything else got cut. The site is small now, and that is the point.

What I learned

Most personal sites die from feature creep. Someone adds a "currently reading" widget, then a Spotify Now Playing, then a guestbook, then a dark-mode toggle, then a comments section, then they stop opening the site at all because every visit becomes maintenance.

The fix is to ask, of every section: would I miss this if it disappeared? If the answer is no, it goes. If the answer is "I built it once and it would be a shame," it goes anyway.

What's left is what matters. What's left is a working document.
